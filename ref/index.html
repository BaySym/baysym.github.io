UoL Referencing Guide

A simplified version of the UoL's Referencing Handbook.

In-text citations

(Weir, 2011) for publications with a single author

(Gaiman and Pratchett, 1990) for publications with two authors

(Stevenson et al., 1886) for publications with more than two authors

(Feynman, 1961) when referring to the whole publication

(Feynman, 1961, 42) when referring to a single page

(Feynman, 1961, 42-45) when referring to a range of pages

Books

Author (Year) Title. Place of Publication: Publisher.
e.g. Goldacre, B. (2009) Bad science. London: Fourth Estate.

Journals

Author (Year) Title of the article. Title of the journal, Volume and issue number, Page range.
e.g. Seymour, K. (2015) Politics and positionality: engaging with maps of meaning. Social Work Education: an International Journal, 34(3) 275-285.

Lectures and seminars

Lecturer (Year) Title. [lecture/seminar]. Module title and code, Edcuation institution, Date of lecture/seminar, Available from [Date of access].
e.g. Ortega, M. (2014) Use of CASP tools in critical analysis [lecture]. Independent Study NUR3021M-1415, University of Lincoln, 2 October. Available from https://blackboard.lincoln.ac.uk/webapps/blackboard/content/listContentEditable.jsp?content_id=_863481_1&course_id=_79737_1&mode=reset [accessed 29 June 2015].

Theses and dissertations

Author (Year) Title. Qualification level. Higher education institution. Available from [Date of access]
e.g. Casey, S. (2011) Tagging amongst friends: an exploration of social media exchange on mobile devices. PhD. University of Lincoln. Available from http://eprints.lincoln.ac.uk/4924/ [accessed 25 June 2015].

Websites

Professional body (Year) Title. Place of publication: Publisher. Available from [Date of access]
Nursing and Midwifery Council (2015) The code for nurses and midwives. London: Nursing and Midwifery Council. Available from http://www.nmc.org.uk/globalassets/sitedocuments/nmc-publications/revised-new-nmc-code.pdf [accessed 29 June 2015].

Extra information

If there is no author, use the publication title instead.

List authors by their surnames, followed by their initials (Enid Blyton should be listed as Blyton E.).

Do not use the format above for corporate authors (Tate Modern should be listed as Tate Modern).

If the source has two authors, separate their names with 'and'. If there are more than two authors, separate their names with commas, with 'and' before the last name.

If you refer to an organisation that has changed its name, you should use the name that it was known as at the time of the source's publication.

Company/organisation names which start with words such as 'A', 'An', or 'The' should be alphabetised under the first major word.

If the information source has no date, use "(undated)" instead.

Use lowercase letters in alphabetical order after publication dates to distinguish multiple sources from an author in the same year ((King, 1977a) followed by (King, 1977b)).

Titles of non-journal sources should be in sentence case and italics.

If an information source is not published in the UK, you should also give the country (Boston, USA).

Be consistent with edition number formatting (8th vs eighth).

Be consistent with date formatting (6 July 2015 vs 6th July 2015 vs 6/7/15).

PDFs are usually referenced as a website unless there is a more appropriate source type.

Quoting is copying a short section of text, word for word, directly from an information source into your work. You should:
- use double quotation marks at the beginning and end of the text
- follow the quotation with the in-text citation
- use ellipses (...) if you omit part of the text

Long quotations are generally longer than two lines. You should:
- start the quotation on a new line
- indent the quotation
- follow the quotation with the in-text citation
- start your analysis of the quotation on a new line.
